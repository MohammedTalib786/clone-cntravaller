21 cool homestays for a summer getaway
in India
From Himachal Pradesh to Kashmir, here’s where you can escape the rising temperatures
By Condé Nast Traveller
17 December 2022

As we revel in spring, a very sunny summer is lurking right around the corner, its long, sweltering days beckoning us. The good news is that you can escape the rising temperatures. While Manali and Srinagar are about 12°C these days, Gangtok remains under 25°C. The weather at such locations will keep you cool but it is the homestays there, with their gracious hosts and delicious food, that will ensure you feel cosy and snug. Here are 21 fine homestays for a summer getaway:


Himachal Pradesh

The days are pleasant at 24°C and the nights cool at 15°C in Kangra Valley. The 150-year-old estate is the home of Neeru and her late husband Naveen Bhandari. Sitting about an hour from Dharamshala, this might just be the place for you to get some respite. On 40 acres, there are just two cottages adorned with bougainvillaea, and each with private verandahs that overlook the Dhauladhar range of the Himalayas. There are over 500 books you can choose from at a small library at the estate should you wish to curl up and read by the fireplace. Savour food and juices made from fresh homegrown vegetables and fruits. Stroll around the tea gardens, while you're serenaded by the gurgling of mountain streams. Don't forget to slot in some play time with Casper, Diana, and Brandy, the friendly resident dogs.

Raison D'être, Manali


Around 2013, their search for a place with year-round good weather landed Shalini and Nitin Beri in a small Himachali village called Raison. This is where they set up their house amidst sunflowers, next to a gushing Beas River. Their space and Raison D'être, their four-bedroom guest accommodation, are separated by a garden. The four rooms, divided into two sets, have common sit-outs with a dining area and pantry. From here, you can view the mountains as well as the nearby orchards. Dining is set up al fresco, with jams, chutneys, pickles, and juices made at home with homegrown fruits complementing all your meals. Set up near Manali, the place is usually warmed by clement sunlight during the days, with temperatures dropping to chilly lows during the nights.


For me, tradition becomes significant when it feels relevant, personal, and truthful to who I am today. It is something that has a lineage; that is evolving with each person who passes it down.


What does it mean to you to be an Indian woman in a globalised world?

As much as my roots mean a lot to me, it’s hard to quantify what it means to be an Indian woman in a globalised world because I also feel like I am other things. I don’t generally label myself as this or that because I think those boxes sometimes limit us. I am Indian but I am also other things.


What is the first thing—a sight, sound, or smell—that comes to mind when you think of India?

It’s hard to answer a question like this because it can be answered in so many ways. Sometimes, when I think of India, I might think of the fruit I had as a child in South India, or the colours that I saw for the first time when I visited Rajasthan. Sometimes, I might think of my home in Delhi where I grew up as a child. One of my favourites though is remembering throughout my life various romantic and sensory experiences in the rain. I always passionately loved the monsoon and the concept of the monsoon. When I say romantic I don’t necessarily mean with someone else. But the incredible romantic flowering that happens within my own heart when I see the air shift, and the colours change, and the rain fall the way it does, and that sensuous feeling on my own skin. As a teenager, one of the most exciting things for me was if I was going to get to come back to India during the monsoon.

What are your favourite childhood memories of India?

I think my favourite childhood memories are of ‘Thayir Shaadam’ with cousins and family and being in Chennai or Bangalore. In Bangalore, my aunt, my mum’s sister had many dogs and I remember just playing with the puppies and being really happy. 



Which place in India do you have the strongest musical memory attached to?

In India, our old house in Delhi—the home we had when I was a child. I started playing there and I had hundreds of daily lessons there. Over the years, I grew up listening to music and meeting great musical legends at our home. My parents even hosted some concerts in the garden when I was a child. So it feels very evocative of music.


What has been the biggest surprise and challenge on your journey as a global Indian artiste?

I suppose the biggest surprise is purely that I've been able to make music that feels true to what my heart desires and that there is a listenership for it. That really blows me away. When I meet people who come up to me and say what my music means to them, that is a surprise to me in a beautiful way every time… I suppose the biggest challenge continues to be working against my own self-doubts and my own inner critic. That is so inhibiting to creativity. When I’m on my own, left alone to make music, the voice in my head says I've got nothing to say. And so I’m learning to trust the creative process and learning to lovingly put aside that negative voice. I'm not always very good at it and that is something that I continue to try and grow through.


What always makes you feel at home when you’re travelling?

I'm a real fan of creature comforts. I've been known to even travel with a favourite mug when I'm travelling for a long time. There's something comfortable but also soothing and ritualistic about something like having a warm beautiful mug to cut my hands around in the morning or in the evening. To connect with myself, I travel with a journal. Maybe sometimes, a favourite shawl that I may drape in a hotel room.


How would you define your sense of style and how much has it been influenced by your travels?

I'm quite seasonal. I lean into a kind of hippie reference. Very flowing. Feminine style in spring and summer, whereas in autumn and winter, I really like a hard edge and lots of black and sharp lines. But I also like to mix things up a little bit. I might put on a very Indian shawl with those really hard lines or I might wear my biker boots in the summer. I like throwing in a little something that gives a little contrast and difference.


Shangraff Mountain House, Srinagar

Breathe in the crisp morning air as you curl up with a book by the central fireplace, or take your morning tea in the garden. Ideal for nature lovers, this charming home is just a 10-minute drive from Dal Lake.


The Mudhouse, Marayoor

About half an hour away from Munnar, this homestay offers an earthy experience with thatched roofs and mud walls. Its two cottages and adjacent treehouse accommodate six people and the grounds house vast sugarcane plantations. The mighty Sahyadris are visible from the cottages and there’s a sandalwood forest nearby.


Is there one composition that you could say best describes your relationship with India?

My song ‘Reunion’, I won't over analyse that and stick to it.
