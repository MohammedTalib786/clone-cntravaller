China is crawling back onto the travel map, one step at a time. In a bid to ease its Covid restrictions, the country is now welcoming tourists and other foreigners by adjusting its visa and entry policies. A notice released on 14 March said that visa services in China will resume from 15 March. As per the notice, these changes have come into effect from 12am on 15 March, Beijing time.


Here are the changes in the visa policy:

1. Visas issued before 28 March 2020 will resume function, as long as they are still within their period of validity.
2. Foreigners will now be able to apply for visas to China, as visa-issuing authorities abroad will resume reviewing and issuing applications.
3. Port visas will also be issued as per laws and regulations.
4. Visa-free entry to Hainan province; to Shanghai for cruises; to Guangdong province for foreigners from Hong Kong and Macao; and to Guilin for ASEAN tourist groups will resume.

The move comes after China suddenly scrapped its Covid restrictions in November and ended mandatory quarantine for inbound travellers in January. Before that, the country had practised a zero-Covid policy with strict screening measures in place. The frequency of passenger flights had dropped dramatically during that time, and tourist visas were suspended altogether as part of the blanket ban on visas. In the past few months, China has resuscitated various facilities such as professional visas for foreign nationals. Now, the country has begun opening outbound group travel in phases by allowing the sale of group tours and package deals. The second phase of this programme, with 40 new countries added to the list of outbound travel destinations, will also roll out from today as well.

What does it mean to you to be an Indian woman in a globalised world?

As much as my roots mean a lot to me, it’s hard to quantify what it means to be an Indian woman in a globalised world because I also feel like I am other things. I don’t generally label myself as this or that because I think those boxes sometimes limit us. I am Indian but I am also other things.


What is the first thing—a sight, sound, or smell—that comes to mind when you think of India?

It’s hard to answer a question like this because it can be answered in so many ways. Sometimes, when I think of India, I might think of the fruit I had as a child in South India, or the colours that I saw for the first time when I visited Rajasthan. Sometimes, I might think of my home in Delhi where I grew up as a child. One of my favourites though is remembering throughout my life various romantic and sensory experiences in the rain. I always passionately loved the monsoon and the concept of the monsoon. When I say romantic I don’t necessarily mean with someone else. But the incredible romantic flowering that happens within my own heart when I see the air shift, and the colours change, and the rain fall the way it does, and that sensuous feeling on my own skin. As a teenager, one of the most exciting things for me was if I was going to get to come back to India during the monsoon.
